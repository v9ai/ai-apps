## Search Intent
Informational — architects and senior engineers designing multi-agent systems want to know how to coordinate autonomous agents. The "what it learned" framing promises hard-won lessons, not theory.

## Competitive Landscape
| Rank | Title | Domain | Format | Word Count | Gap |

| 1 | Complete Guide to Agentic Coding | teamday.ai | guide | ~3000 | General, no strategic coordination |

## Recommended Structure
- **Format**: Direct statement / lessons learned
- **Word count**: 1500-2000
- **Title tag**: "We Built a Strategic Brain for Our AI Pipeline. Here's What It Learned."
- **Meta description**: "Six agents can mine, audit, evolve, fix, and verify code. None knows when to do any of it. We built the coordinator that decides — with phase detection, cost-aware routing, and a kill switch."
- **H1**: We Built a Strategic Brain for Our AI Pipeline. Here's What It Learned.
- **H2s**:
  1. Five Agents, Zero Coordination — the problem with leaderless multi-agent systems
  2. Six Papers That Shaped the Strategic Brain — ROMA, DyTopo, CASTER, MonoScale, Phase Transition, Bayesian Orchestration
  3. Three Phases — improvement, saturation, collapse risk
  4. Dynamic Routing — not every cycle uses every agent
  5. When to Ask for Help — the most important feature

## Internal Linking Opportunities
- All 5 other articles → the Meta-Optimizer coordinates them all

## Differentiation Strategy
"Strategic brain" framing is unique — most multi-agent content covers the workers, not the coordinator. The "when to stop" angle addresses the biggest gap in self-improving systems. Gartner's 1,445% surge in multi-agent inquiries validates market interest.

## Distribution Notes
- HackerNews: "when to stop improving" is a nuanced take the community values
- Twitter/X: the three phases (improvement/saturation/collapse) as a standalone framework
- LinkedIn: the strategic coordination angle resonates with engineering leaders

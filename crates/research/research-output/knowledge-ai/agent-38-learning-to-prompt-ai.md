## 1. Prompt Engineering as a Learning Skill: Frameworks and Taxonomies

### 1.1 Defining Prompt Literacy
**Prompt literacy** represents the ability to communicate effectively with generative AI systems through natural language commands. As defined by **Maloy & Gattupalli (2024)** [Prompt Literacy](https://doi.org/10.59668/371.14442), this skill enables users to "direct generative AI to achieve defined objectives, while exercising judgment and responsibility." Unlike traditional programming, prompt literacy operates at the human-AI interface level, translating human intent into AI-compatible directives.

### 1.2 Competency-Based Frameworks
The **Generative AI Literacy Framework** by **Annapureddy et al. (2024)** [Generative AI Literacy: Twelve Defining Competencies](https://doi.org/10.1145/3685680) identifies 12 essential competencies organized into four progressive levels:

**Level 1: Foundational AI Literacy**
1. Understanding basic AI concepts and capabilities
2. Recognizing AI limitations and failure modes
3. Awareness of ethical considerations

**Level 2: Operational Competence**
4. Basic prompt formulation and refinement
5. Understanding different AI model capabilities
6. Managing AI interactions and context

**Level 3: Advanced Application**
7. Complex prompt engineering techniques
8. Integration of AI into workflows
9. Evaluation and validation of AI outputs

**Level 4: Strategic Leadership**
10. Ethical AI deployment and governance
11. AI system design and customization
12. Teaching and mentoring AI literacy

### 1.3 Prompt Literacy Characteristics
Research by **Kim & Seo (2024)** [KFL Learners' Use of ChatGPT: An Analysis of Prompt Literacy in Writing Assignments](https://research-repository.uwa.edu.au/en/publications/740d83ae-bf1c-4e3e-9411-9065f72ac197) identifies three key characteristics of learner prompt literacy:

1. **Uniformity**: Tendency to use limited, repetitive prompt patterns
2. **Discreteness**: Treating prompts as isolated commands rather than conversational exchanges
3. **Passivity**: Limited initiative in refining or expanding prompts based on AI responses

### 1.4 Developmental Trajectories
Studies show that prompt literacy develops through distinct stages:

**Novice Stage**: Simple, direct commands with limited refinement
**Intermediate Stage**: Structured prompts with clear specifications and constraints
**Advanced Stage**: Conversational, iterative prompting with strategic goal decomposition
**Expert Stage**: Meta-prompting and system-level interaction design

## 3. Critical Evaluation of AI Output: Fact-Checking and Bias Detection

### 3.1 Critical AI Literacy Framework
**Lintner (2024)** [A systematic review of AI literacy scales](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41539-024-00264-4) synthesizes assessment frameworks for critical AI evaluation skills, identifying four core dimensions:

**Cognitive Dimension:**
- Understanding AI limitations and failure modes
- Recognizing hallucination patterns
- Assessing output credibility and reliability

**Procedural Dimension:**
- Systematic fact-checking procedures
- Cross-referencing with authoritative sources
- Validation through multiple AI systems

**Ethical Dimension:**
- Bias detection and analysis
- Privacy and security assessment
- Ethical implication evaluation

**Metacognitive Dimension:**
- Self-monitoring of reliance on AI
- Critical reflection on decision processes
- Adjustment of trust calibration

### 3.2 Fact-Checking Protocols
Research identifies systematic approaches to AI output validation:

**Multi-Source Verification Protocol:**
1. **Primary verification**: Cross-reference with authoritative sources
2. **Secondary verification**: Consult multiple AI systems
3. **Tertiary verification**: Expert consultation when available
4. **Process verification**: Trace AI reasoning where possible

**Critical Questioning Framework:**
- What evidence supports this claim?
- What alternative explanations exist?
- What limitations might affect this conclusion?
- How might bias influence this output?

### 3.3 Bias Detection and Mitigation
**Ntoutsi et al. (2020)** [Bias in data-driven artificial intelligence systems—An introductory survey](https://doi.org/10.1002/widm.1356) provides foundational understanding of AI bias, while educational applications focus on:

- Detecting ideological framing in generated content
- Understanding statistical bias in recommendations

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- Diverse prompt formulation to test for bias

- Critical analysis of AI decision processes
- Ethical reflection on bias implications

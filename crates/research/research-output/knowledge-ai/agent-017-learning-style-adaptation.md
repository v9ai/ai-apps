# Learning Style Adaptation and Individual Differences: Evidence-Based Approaches (2018-2026)

## Executive Summary

This research survey examines the evolution from learning style myths to evidence-based individual differences in adaptive instruction. The analysis covers the learning styles debate, evidence-based individual differences, aptitude-treatment interactions, learner preference modeling, and AI-driven Universal Design for Learning (UDL) approaches.

## 1. The Learning Styles Debate: From VARK/Kolb to Neuromyths

### The Persistence of Learning Style Myths

**Μαριέττα Παπαδάτου-Παστού et al. (2020)** [The learning styles neuromyth: when the same term means different things to different teachers](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10212-020-00485-2) reveals that despite recognition as a neuromyth, learning styles remain prevalent in education. The study found that education professionals conceptualize learning styles using completely different notions and categorizations, leading to inconsistent implementation.

**Karoline Aslaksen & Håvard Lorås (2018)** [The Modality-Specific Learning Style Hypothesis: A Mini-Review](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2018.01538) concludes that there is no evidence to support the meshing hypothesis (matching instruction to modality-specific learning style preferences) and that it represents a persistent neuromyth in education.

### Evidence Against Learning Style Effectiveness

**Stephen B. R. E. Brown (2023)** [The persistence of matching teaching and learning styles: A review of the ubiquity of this neuromyth, predictors of its endorsement, and recommendations to end it](https://doi.org/10.3389/feduc.2023.1147498) systematically reviews the evidence against the meshing hypothesis, finding that formal statistical tests consistently fail to support crossover interaction effects that would validate learning style matching.

**Xin Sun et al. (2023)** [Beware the myth: learning styles affect parents', children's, and teachers' thinking about children's academic potential](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41539-023-00190-x) demonstrates that learning style labels influence perceptions of academic potential, with visual learners being judged as more intelligent than hands-on learners, despite no empirical basis for such judgments.

## 2. Evidence-Based Individual Differences

### Cognitive Load Theory and Working Memory

**Ton de Jong (2009)** [Cognitive load theory, educational research, and instructional design: some food for thought](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11251-009-9110-0) establishes the foundational principle that cognitive capacity in working memory is limited, and instructional systems should optimize working memory use to avoid cognitive overload.

**Melina Klepsch & Tina Seufert (2020)** [Understanding instructional design effects by differentiated measurement of intrinsic, extraneous, and germane cognitive load](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11251-020-09502-9) advances cognitive load measurement, providing tools to distinguish different types of cognitive load for more precise instructional adaptation.

### Prior Knowledge and Expertise Reversal Effect

**Slava Kalyuga & Alexander Renkl (2009)** [Expertise reversal effect and its instructional implications: introduction to the special issue](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11251-009-9102-0) documents how instructional methods that are effective for novices can become ineffective or even detrimental for more expert learners, highlighting the need for dynamic adaptation based on prior knowledge.

### Working Memory Capacity and Individual Differences

**Janina Lehmann et al. (2015)** [Working memory capacity and disfluency effect: an aptitude-treatment-interaction study](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11409-015-9149-z) demonstrates that the disfluency effect (where less legible texts lead to better learning) interacts with working memory capacity, showing differential effects based on individual cognitive abilities.

## 3. Aptitude-Treatment Interactions (ATI) and Adaptive Instruction

### Dynamic Framework for Personalized Education

**Leonard Tetzlaff et al. (2020)** [Developing Personalized Education: A Dynamic Framework](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-020-09570-w) proposes a comprehensive framework for personalized education that integrates cognitive, affective, and behavioral factors, emphasizing dynamic adaptation rather than static learner categorization.

### ATI in Multimedia Learning

**Janina Lehmann & Tina Seufert (2020)** [The Interaction Between Text Modality and the Learner's Modality Preference Influences Comprehension and Cognitive Load](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2019.02820) tests the aptitude-treatment interaction between text modality and learners' modality preference, finding complex interactions that challenge simple matching approaches.

### Executive Functions as Moderators

**Matthias Schwaighofer et al. (2017)** [Executive functions in the context of complex learning: Malleable moderators?](https://doi.org/10.14786/flr.v5i1.268) argues that executive functions can moderate the effectiveness of instructional approaches and suggests that engagement in complex activities may enhance these functions.

## 4. Learner Preference Modeling: What Actually Matters

### Beyond Learning Styles: Evidence-Based Preferences

Research indicates that while learning style matching lacks empirical support, other learner characteristics do matter for adaptation:

**1. Prior Knowledge Level**: The most robust predictor of instructional effectiveness
**2. Working Memory Capacity**: Affects ability to handle complex instructional formats
**3. Motivation and Self-Regulation**: Critical for engagement and persistence
**4. Metacognitive Awareness**: Influences ability to monitor and regulate learning
**5. Specific Cognitive Abilities**: Domain-specific aptitudes that predict learning success

### Modern Learner Modeling Approaches

**Eduardo Oliveira et al. (2021)** [Enabling adaptive, personalised and context-aware interaction in a smart learning environment: Piloting the iCollab system](https://doi.org/10.14742/ajet.6792) introduces the iCollab platform, which uses adaptive environments to tailor learning experiences based on multiple learner characteristics rather than simplistic learning style categorizations.

## 5. Universal Design for Learning (UDL) and AI-Driven Flexibility

### AI-Enhanced UDL Implementation

**Maryam Roshanaei et al. (2023)** [Harnessing AI to Foster Equity in Education: Opportunities, Challenges, and Emerging Strategies](https://doi.org/10.4236/jilsa.2023.154009) explores how AI can promote equity through personalized learning, analyzing learning patterns to customize educational content for individual needs.

**Shalece Kohnke & Tiffanie Zaugg (2025)** [Artificial Intelligence: An Untapped Opportunity for Equity and Access in STEM Education](https://doi.org/10.3390/educsci15010068) examines how AI can address barriers for students with disabilities through adaptive learning platforms and intelligent tutors that support STEM practices.

### Inclusive Adaptive Learning Systems

**Sandeep Lopez (2025)** [Integrating Artificial Intelligence in Regional Education: A Holistic Framework for Inclusive, Adaptive, and Community-Centric Learning](https://doi.org/10.47362/ejsss.2025.6102) proposes a multidimensional framework incorporating localized content development, adaptive learning systems, teacher support tools, and inclusion features guided by UDL principles.

**Chima Abimbola Eden et al. (2024)** [A review of AI-driven pedagogical strategies for equitable access to science education](https://doi.org/10.30574/msarr.2024.10.2.0043) reviews AI-driven strategies for improving equitable access to science education, emphasizing adaptive systems that respond to diverse learner needs.

## 6. AI-Driven Adaptive Learning Systems (2018-2026)

### Production Systems and Implementation

**Hyangdong Kim et al. (2025)** [Effect of AI-Based Adaptive Learning Class: Fundamental of Nursing Medication Calculation](https://doi.org/10.22251/jlcci.2025.25.9.299) demonstrates practical implementation of AI-based adaptive learning in nursing education, showing positive effects on medication calculation performance.

**Jin Sook Kan et al. (2023)** [The Impact of AI-Adaptive Learning on TOEIC Academic Achievement and Self-Directed Learning Competency in College English Classes](https://doi.org/10.22251/jlcci.2023.23.19.267) validates the efficacy of AI-adaptive learning in college English courses, showing improvements in both academic achievement and self-directed learning competencies.

### Self-Regulated Learning Support

**Jeong Yoon Lee et al. (2025)** [An Analysis of Self-regulated Learning Features of the Adaptive Learning Platform for K-12 Education](https://doi.org/10.22251/jlcci.2025.25.2.759) analyzes features that support self-regulated learning within AI-enabled adaptive learning platforms for K-12 education, providing design implications for AI digital textbooks.

## 7. Key Research Gaps and Future Directions

### Current Limitations

1. **Overemphasis on Learning Styles**: Despite evidence against their effectiveness, learning styles continue to dominate educational discourse
2. **Limited Integration of Multiple Factors**: Most systems focus on single dimensions rather than integrated learner profiles
3. **Ethical Concerns in AI Adaptation**: Potential for bias and discrimination in algorithmic decision-making
4. **Teacher Training Gap**: Insufficient preparation for evidence-based adaptive instruction
5. **Cultural Context Neglect**: Most research based on Western, educated, industrialized, rich, democratic (WEIRD) populations

### Emerging Research Areas

1. **Multimodal Learning Analytics**: Combining physiological, behavioral, and cognitive data
2. **Explainable AI for Education**: Making adaptive decisions transparent to stakeholders
3. **Cross-Cultural Adaptation**: Developing culturally responsive adaptive systems
4. **Lifelong Learning Models**: Tracking and adapting to changing learner characteristics over time
5. **Human-AI Collaboration**: Optimal division of labor between teachers and adaptive systems

## 8. Practical Implications for Educational Design

### Evidence-Based Recommendations

1. **Focus on Prior Knowledge**: Use diagnostic assessments to determine starting points
2. **Consider Cognitive Load**: Design instruction that optimizes working memory use
3. **Support Self-Regulation**: Build metacognitive and motivational support into systems
4. **Implement UDL Principles**: Provide multiple means of representation, action/expression, and engagement
5. **Use Dynamic Adaptation**: Continuously adjust based on learner progress and needs

### Teacher Professional Development

**Cordelia Menz et al. (2020)** [Misconceptions die hard: prevalence and reduction of wrong beliefs in topics from educational psychology among preservice teachers](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10212-020-00474-5) highlights the persistence of educational misconceptions and the need for targeted interventions in teacher education programs.

## Conclusion

The field has evolved from simplistic learning style matching to sophisticated, evidence-based approaches to individual differences. Modern adaptive learning systems leverage AI and UDL principles to create flexible, inclusive learning environments that respond to multiple dimensions of learner variability. Future research should focus on integrating cognitive, affective, and contextual factors in dynamic adaptation models while addressing ethical concerns and ensuring equitable access.

## 1. AI Literacy Frameworks: What Should Students Know About AI?

### 1.1 Conceptual Foundations of AI Literacy

**Definition and Scope:**
AI literacy encompasses the knowledge, skills, and attitudes needed to understand, use, and critically evaluate AI technologies. Recent frameworks emphasize a multidimensional approach:

**Chiu et al. (2024)** propose a comprehensive framework distinguishing between AI literacy (basic understanding) and AI competency (application skills). Their framework identifies four key dimensions:
1. **Technical Understanding**: Basic AI concepts, algorithms, and capabilities
2. **Critical Evaluation**: Assessing AI outputs, recognizing biases, understanding limitations
3. **Practical Application**: Effective use of AI tools, prompt engineering, integration into workflows
4. **Ethical Awareness**: Privacy, fairness, accountability, and societal impacts

**Ng et al. (2023)** developed the AI Literacy Questionnaire (AILQ) measuring four dimensions:
- **Affective**: Motivation, attitudes, and self-efficacy
- **Behavioral**: Actual use and application of AI tools
- **Cognitive**: Knowledge and understanding of AI concepts
- **Ethical**: Awareness of ethical implications and responsible use

### 1.2 Core Competency Frameworks

**Casal Otero et al. (2023)** conducted a systematic review of K-12 AI literacy, identifying key competencies:
- Understanding AI concepts and applications
- Recognizing AI's capabilities and limitations
- Developing critical thinking about AI
- Understanding ethical implications
- Developing computational thinking skills

**Walter (2024)** emphasizes the importance of prompt engineering proficiency and critical thinking skills as essential components of AI literacy in educational settings.

## 2. K-12 AI Education: Age-Appropriate Concepts and Activities

### 2.1 Developmental Progression

**Elementary Level (Grades K-5):**
- **Conceptual Understanding**: Basic AI concepts through analogies and stories
- **Activities**: Unplugged activities, pattern recognition games, simple robotics
- **Tools**: Age-appropriate programming environments (Scratch Jr., Blockly)

**Middle School (Grades 6-8):**
- **Conceptual Understanding**: Machine learning basics, data collection, simple algorithms
- **Activities**: Data collection projects, basic chatbot creation, ethical discussions
- **Tools**: Visual programming with AI extensions, simple ML platforms

**High School (Grades 9-12):**
- **Conceptual Understanding**: Advanced ML concepts, neural networks, ethical implications
- **Activities**: Real-world AI projects, bias detection, AI ethics debates
- **Tools**: Python programming, TensorFlow, Jupyter notebooks

### 2.2 Pedagogical Approaches

**Zhou et al. (2020)** propose a design framework for K-12 AI learning experiences emphasizing:
- **Project-Based Learning**: Real-world problem-solving with AI
- **Inquiry-Based Learning**: Exploring AI concepts through questioning
- **Collaborative Learning**: Group projects and peer learning
- **Reflective Practice**: Critical thinking about AI impacts

**Henry et al. (2021)** developed role-playing games to teach AI concepts, making abstract concepts tangible through interactive experiences.

### 2.3 Assessment and Evaluation

**Zhong & Liu (2024)** developed an AI literacy assessment scale for secondary students, providing validated instruments for measuring learning outcomes across cognitive, affective, and behavioral dimensions.

## 4. Teacher AI Literacy: Professional Development for Educators

### 4.1 Teacher Competency Frameworks

**Ng et al. (2023)** identify key competencies for teachers' AI digital literacy:
- **Technical Proficiency**: Understanding and using AI tools
- **Pedagogical Integration**: Incorporating AI into teaching practices
- **Ethical Awareness**: Addressing AI ethics in educational contexts
- **Assessment Literacy**: Evaluating student work with AI assistance

**Black et al. (2024)** propose a framework for AI education in Educator Preparation Programs (EPPs):
1. **Foundational Knowledge**: Basic AI concepts and applications
2. **Pedagogical Integration**: Strategies for teaching with and about AI
3. **Ethical Considerations**: Addressing AI ethics in K-12 contexts
4. **Professional Development**: Continuous learning and adaptation

### 4.2 Professional Development Models

**Kim et al. (2022)** identify teacher perspectives on requirements for effective student-AI collaboration:
- **Curriculum Design**: Structured learning pathways
- **Student-AI Interaction**: Guided engagement with AI tools
- **Learning Environments**: Supportive infrastructure and resources
- **Assessment Strategies**: Appropriate evaluation methods

**Key Professional Development Components:**
- **Workshops and Training**: Hands-on experience with AI tools
- **Curriculum Co-design**: Collaborative development of AI-integrated lessons
- **Peer Learning Communities**: Sharing best practices and challenges
- **Continuous Support**: Ongoing mentoring and resources

### 4.3 Challenges and Barriers

**Velander et al. (2023)** identify challenges in Swedish teachers' AI implementation:
- **Technical Barriers**: Lack of infrastructure and tools
- **Knowledge Gaps**: Limited understanding of AI concepts
- **Time Constraints**: Insufficient time for training and implementation
- **Ethical Concerns**: Uncertainty about appropriate AI use

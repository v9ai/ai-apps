Chapter Two: The Big Picture, What You Built

Let's talk about what you actually built, and let's do it at three levels of depth, because depending on how Vienna frames her questions, you may need to explain it simply or go deep.

At the most basic level, here is what the BS Detector does. It reads a legal brief, specifically a Motion for Summary Judgment, or MSJ, which is a lawyer's written argument asking a judge to decide a case without a trial. The system checks whether the lawyer is telling the truth about two things. First, are the legal cases the lawyer cites real cases, and do those cases actually say what the lawyer claims they say? Second, do the facts in the brief match what is in the supporting documents, things like the police report, the medical records, and the witness statements? After checking both of those things, the system writes a report telling the judge what it found.

At an intermediate level, here is what is going on under the hood. The system is a multi-agent pipeline built in Python with FastAPI. Five specialized AI agents, a document parser, a citation verifier, a fact checker, a report synthesizer, and a judicial memo writer, process legal documents in a defined sequence with one parallelization point. All the data passed between agents is typed with Pydantic models. The pipeline can run on DeepSeek in the cloud or Ollama locally through LangChain's ChatOpenAI abstraction. And there is an evaluation harness with thirty-one assertions across six dimensions that measures output quality against eight planted ground-truth errors.

At the expert level, this is a sequential-parallel directed acyclic graph of five language-model-backed agents orchestrated through a pipeline orchestrator that is itself fully deterministic, making zero language model calls. The only concurrent execution point is an asyncio gather call at the orchestrator level that runs citation verification and fact checking in parallel, exploiting the independence between those two tasks. Within the citation verifier, there is a second level of parallelism where individual citations are verified concurrently. All inter-agent contracts are Pydantic-serialized dictionaries, not object references, which makes the pipeline distributable without architectural changes. The eval harness implements a dual-matching strategy combining keyword-based matching with an optional language-model-as-judge semantic evaluation, plus evidence grounding checks and SQLite-persisted run history keyed by git commit hash. The core architectural gap is that citation verification relies on language model parametric knowledge rather than a legal database. The case context injection mechanism is a nineteen-line placeholder for what would be a retrieval-augmented generation pipeline over a curated legal corpus in production.

Now let's talk about how documents actually flow through the system, because this is the backbone of everything. A set of legal documents enters the pipeline. The document parser reads the Motion for Summary Judgment and extracts two things: every legal citation it can find, and every factual claim. Think of the parser as a meticulous legal assistant going through the brief with a highlighter, marking every case citation and every factual assertion.

Once the parser finishes, two things happen at the same time. The citation verifier takes all those extracted citations and checks each one. Is this a real case? Does it actually say what the lawyer claims? Does it apply in this jurisdiction? Meanwhile, the fact checker takes all four documents, the MSJ, the police report, the medical records, and the witness statement, and compares them side by side across eight specific categories, looking for contradictions. These two agents run in parallel because they are completely independent. The citation verifier does not need anything from the fact checker, and the fact checker does not need anything from the citation verifier.

After both finish, the report synthesizer receives all of their results and does editorial work. It identifies the most important problems, ranks them by severity, assigns confidence scores, and filters out the noise. A judge does not want to read eighteen entries to find six actual problems. The synthesizer surfaces what matters.

Finally, the judicial memo agent takes the synthesizer's output and writes a formal memo in legal language, the kind of document a law clerk would prepare for a judge. It includes a prose summary, a list of key issues, recommended actions, and an overall assessment.

Now, why multi-agent instead of a single prompt? This is a question you should expect, and the answer goes to the heart of why this architecture works. When you ask a single language model to simultaneously extract citations, verify their accuracy, cross-reference facts across four documents, synthesize findings, and write a judicial memo, each objective gets partial attention. Language model attention is finite, and task-switching degrades quality. The extraction task needs the instruction be comprehensive, find everything. The verification task needs be skeptical, question everything. The synthesis task needs be concise, surface only what matters. The memo task needs be persuasive, write in formal legal language. These are fundamentally different cognitive framings, and a single prompt cannot hold all of them at once without diluting each one.

The empirical evidence backs this up. The single-prompt version of this system caught three to four of eight planted errors. The multi-agent version catches six to eight. That improvement came from the architecture change, not from prompt tuning. Splitting the work into specialized agents, each with a focused instruction, produced measurably better results.

Think of it like an assembly line in a factory. You would not ask one worker to mine the ore, smelt the steel, machine the parts, assemble the engine, and paint the car. Each of those tasks requires different skills, different tools, and different mindsets. The same principle applies here. Each agent is a specialist that does one thing well.

Now, why exactly five agents? Why not three, why not seven? This is the Pareto optimum between specialization and coordination overhead. With three agents, you would have to combine tasks. Maybe you merge the parser and verifier, or merge the synthesizer and memo writer. But combining extraction with verification means the same language model call has to be both comprehensive and skeptical at the same time, which produces worse results at both tasks. With seven or more agents, you could further specialize. Maybe separate agents for date checking, safety equipment checking, jurisdiction checking, and so on. But each additional agent adds a language model call that takes three to ten seconds, adds a failure point, and adds a coordination step. The five-agent decomposition maps cleanly to five distinct cognitive tasks: extract, verify citations, verify facts, aggregate, and communicate. Fewer agents conflate these tasks. More agents add overhead without meaningful specialization gains. Five is the sweet spot.


Chapter Three: Architecture Deep Dive

Now let's go deeper into the architecture, because this is where the interview will spend the most time.

The system has five agents plus an orchestrator, and it is important to understand that the orchestrator is not an agent. The orchestrator is a coordinator. It makes zero language model calls. Its behavior is fully deterministic. The only non-determinism in the entire pipeline comes from the agents themselves, through their interactions with the language model. This separation is not an accident. It is a deliberate and important design choice.

Here is why the orchestrator is not an agent. If the orchestrator made language model calls, say, to decide which agents to invoke, or to route findings between agents, or to determine whether a particular brief needs extra analysis, then the pipeline's behavior would become unpredictable. Different briefs would get different analytical treatment depending on what the language model decided in the moment. That makes evaluation results incomparable across runs. It makes debugging a nightmare, because you cannot reproduce a failure if the control flow itself is non-deterministic. In a legal system, you need a guarantee: every brief, every time, goes through the same pipeline. Parser, then citation verifier and fact checker in parallel, then synthesizer, then memo. No exceptions. The deterministic orchestrator provides that guarantee.

Let's walk through each agent. The Document Parser Agent reads the Motion for Summary Judgment and extracts citations and facts. It makes a single language model call with an extraction prompt. The key design insight here is that extraction is a different cognitive task from verification. When you ask a language model to simultaneously extract citations and verify them, it tends to skip citations it considers unimportant. But unimportant citations might be exactly the ones worth checking, like an out-of-state citation buried in a footnote. By separating extraction, the parser's instruction is simply find everything. Completeness is its only job.

The Citation Verifier Agent takes those extracted citations and checks each one. It implements per-citation parallelism, meaning each citation gets its own language model call running concurrently. With five to ten citations in a typical MSJ, sequential verification would take fifteen to fifty seconds. Parallel verification completes in three to ten seconds, because all the calls happen at the same time. The citations are independent. Verifying one case has nothing to do with verifying another.

The Fact Checker Agent reads all four documents in a single prompt and checks them against eight specific categories: date consistency, personal protective equipment or PPE safety, work control, scaffolding condition, OSHA compliance, injury details, statute of limitations, and strategic omission. These categories are ordered by objectivity. Dates come first because a date either matches or it does not. Strategic omissions come last because they require the most subjective judgment. This ordering gives the language model a warm-up of easy, high-confidence tasks before tackling the harder calls.

The Report Synthesizer Agent receives the results from both the citation verifier and the fact checker, and its job is editorial. It identifies the most important problems, ranks them by severity, assigns confidence scores at multiple levels, and filters out noise. Raw verification results include a lot of confirmations, things that checked out fine, and a judge does not need to see those. The synthesizer surfaces only what matters.

The Judicial Memo Agent takes the synthesizer's output and writes a formal memo suitable for a judge. This is separated from synthesis because analytical work and persuasive legal writing require different prompting strategies. A language model prompted for analysis produces better analysis when it is not simultaneously trying to write polished prose. And a language model prompted for legal writing produces more fluent prose when it is not simultaneously doing arithmetic on confidence scores.

Now let's talk about typed contracts between agents, because this is a design choice that has significant implications. All data passed between agents uses Pydantic models, but the data is serialized to dictionaries before being passed. Every agent receives dictionaries and returns dictionaries. This is a deliberate choice over passing Pydantic object instances directly, and there are three reasons why.

First, JSON serializability. When data is in dictionary form, it can be logged, cached, or transmitted over a network without any additional serialization step. You can inspect exactly what was passed between agents just by looking at the logs.

Second, loose coupling. Agents do not import each other's Pydantic models. The parser does not need to know about the Verified Citation model that the citation verifier produces. Each agent defines its own input and output contracts independently.

Third, and this is the big one, future distributability. If you wanted to move agents to separate services, say, citation verification on a GPU node and fact checking on a CPU node, the dictionary-based interface requires no changes whatsoever. Passing Pydantic objects would create import dependencies across service boundaries. Dictionaries are the universal interchange format.

Now, the parallel execution architecture. There are two levels of parallelism in this system. The first level is at the orchestrator. After the parser finishes, the orchestrator launches the citation verifier and the fact checker simultaneously using asyncio gather. It passes return exceptions equals true, which is critical. That flag means if one of those two agents fails entirely, the other one's results are still collected. Without that flag, a single failure would cancel all in-flight tasks. In legal verification, getting results from one agent is strictly better than getting results from zero agents.

The second level of parallelism is inside the citation verifier. Each individual citation is verified concurrently through its own language model call, again using asyncio gather with return exceptions equals true. If one citation's verification times out or fails, the other citations' results are still returned. That failed citation gets a status of Could Not Verify with error details in its notes field. The failure is recorded as data, not as an exception that crashes the system.

Let's trace the data flow through the entire pipeline in detail. The parser receives a dictionary of document keys mapped to their text content. It processes the MSJ and returns a dictionary containing a list of citations and a list of facts. Only the citations are forwarded to the next stage. The facts field is a vestige of an earlier design and is currently unused, but it is harmless and the parser already does the extraction work.

The citation verifier receives those citations plus a case context string that the orchestrator injects. The case context comes from a small module that provides domain-specific knowledge about known cases. For the test case, Rivera versus Harmon, this includes knowledge about the Privette doctrine and the specific citations involved. For unknown cases, it is empty. The verifier returns a list of verified citations, each with a status. Those statuses are Supported, meaning the citation checks out; Not Supported, meaning the citation appears to be wrong; Could Not Verify, meaning the system cannot determine one way or the other; or Misleading, meaning the citation is technically real but mischaracterizes what the case actually held.

Meanwhile, the fact checker receives the full raw documents, not the parser's extracted facts. This is an important distinction. The parser extracts facts from the MSJ specifically, but fact checking requires comparing MSJ claims against the complete text of supporting documents. If the police report contains a detail the parser did not extract as a fact, the fact checker still needs to find it. Raw document access gives the fact checker maximum context. The fact checker returns a list of verified facts, each with its own status: Consistent, Contradictory, Partial, or Could Not Verify.

Both result sets flow to the synthesizer, which truncates them to ten thousand characters each to stay within token limits. The synthesizer produces three things: top findings ranked by severity, confidence scores at multiple levels, and a list of unknown issues. Those findings flow to the memo agent, truncated to six thousand characters. Since findings are pre-sorted by severity, the truncation naturally preserves the most important findings and drops the least important ones.

Now let's talk about the Base Agent pattern, because it provides the foundation that all five agents build on. There is an abstract base class with an execute method that each agent implements. The base class provides shared helpers for calling the language model. One helper returns a validated Pydantic instance by injecting the target model's JSON schema into the system prompt and parsing the response. Another returns raw text. Every language model call in the system is wrapped in an asyncio wait for with a one-hundred-twenty-second timeout. This means no agent can hang indefinitely. If a language model call takes too long, it times out, the error is caught, and the agent returns a degraded but valid response.

The language model calls themselves go through an LLM Service that abstracts away the underlying model provider. The service supports DeepSeek for cloud inference and Ollama running qwen two point five at seven billion parameters for local development. Both are accessed through LangChain's ChatOpenAI abstraction, which provides a uniform interface regardless of which backend is actually handling the request. Swapping to a different model, say GPT-4 or Claude, would be a configuration change. You would change an environment variable, and the rest of the pipeline would not know the difference. However, and this is an honest caveat, prompt engineering is model-specific. Changing models would require re-running the evaluation harness and potentially re-tuning prompts.

The structured output mechanism deserves a mention because it solves a real problem. DeepSeek's OpenAI-compatible API has inconsistent support for native structured output features like response format and function calling. So instead of relying on those features, the system injects the Pydantic model's JSON schema directly into the system prompt, telling the language model exactly what structure to return. Then a three-layer parsing pipeline extracts the JSON from the response, handling markdown code fences, raw JSON, and nested brackets. This approach is more portable across model providers than relying on any one provider's native structured output support.

One final architectural detail that ties everything together: the status tracking system. The orchestrator maintains per-agent status entries with millisecond-precision timing. Each agent transitions through states: pending, running, then either success or failed. Every transition is recorded with timing information and, in the case of failure, error messages. This produces a fully observable pipeline in the final verification report. A judge, or more likely a court technician, can look at the pipeline status field and see exactly which agents ran, how long each one took, and whether any of them failed. This is a lightweight distributed tracing system without the overhead of a full observability platform like OpenTelemetry. It tracks agents, not individual language model calls, because agent-level timing is what matters for pipeline optimization. Knowing that the fact checker took fifteen seconds tells you where to optimize, even if you do not know how those fifteen seconds split across internal operations.


Chapter Four: Agent-by-Agent Walkthrough

Let's slow down and get specific about what each of the five agents actually does, how it does it, and why it was designed the way it was. In an interview, being able to walk through each agent with precision is what separates someone who built the system from someone who generated it and moved on. Think of this chapter as your chance to show architectural fluency.

We start with the DocumentParserAgent. This is the first agent in the pipeline, and its job might sound simple but it encodes a critical design insight. The parser reads the lawyer's motion for summary judgment and extracts two things: every legal citation in the document and every factual claim. It makes a single call to the language model using the citation extraction prompt, and the output is validated against a Pydantic model called ExtractionResult, which contains a list of citations and a list of facts.

Now here is the key question an interviewer might ask: why is extraction a separate agent? Why not just have the citation verifier extract its own citations as it goes? The answer is that extraction and verification are distinct cognitive tasks, and when you ask a large language model to do both simultaneously, it tends to skip citations it considers unimportant. But those "unimportant" citations, the one buried in a footnote, the out-of-state case tucked into a parenthetical, those are often the ones most worth checking. Separating extraction ensures completeness. The parser's instruction is "find everything." The verifier's instruction is "be skeptical about each one." Those are different mental stances, and splitting them across agents produces better results for both.

There is a known limitation worth mentioning. The parser only processes the motion for summary judgment itself. It does not extract facts from the supporting documents like the police report or the medical records. That means the fact checker, which comes later, has to perform both extraction and verification in a single pass, which is a heavier cognitive load for the language model. In a production system you would want separate extraction passes for each document type.

One more expert-level detail: the parser actually extracts both citations and facts, but the orchestrator only uses the citations. The facts field was intended for a cross-reference step that got descoped. The extraction remains in the code as a design artifact, and mentioning this in an interview shows you know the codebase at a granular level.

Also notice that the extraction prompt deliberately does not include the case context placeholder. This is intentional. Extraction should be context-free because you want to extract all citations, including the ones that are perfectly correct. Injecting case-specific knowledge at the extraction stage would bias the parser toward flagging certain citations, which is the verifier's job, not the parser's.

Now let's move on to the CitationVerifierAgent, which is where the architecture gets more interesting. This agent takes each legal citation from the parser and checks three things: Is this a real case? Does the case actually say what the lawyer claims it says? Does this case apply in the relevant jurisdiction? It checks each citation independently and in parallel, like having multiple research assistants each looking up one case at the same time.

The parallelism is implemented with asyncio.gather, which you can find around line 72 of the citation verifier file. Each citation gets its own language model call through a method called verify one. With five to ten citations in a typical motion for summary judgment, sequential verification would take five times three to ten seconds, which is fifteen to fifty seconds. Parallel verification completes in the time of the single slowest call, which is three to ten seconds. The citations are independent. Verifying Privette v. Superior Court does not require knowing anything about Dixon v. Lone Star. This is actually a second level of parallelism in the system. The first level is at the orchestrator, where the citation verifier and the fact checker run concurrently.

The error handling in this agent is worth spending real time on because it demonstrates three-layer error isolation, which is one of the most interview-worthy patterns in the codebase.

Layer one is the inner try-except inside the verify one method, around lines 29 through 58. This catches per-citation language model failures. If one citation times out or returns garbage, the method returns a VerifiedCitation object with COULD NOT VERIFY status and the error details in the notes field. The failure is recorded as data, not as an exception.

Layer two is the return exceptions equals true flag on the asyncio.gather call at lines 72 through 75. This means that if one citation's verification fails in a way that escapes the inner try-except, which should not happen but might in edge cases like serialization errors, the other citations' results are still collected. Without this flag, a single failure would cancel all in-flight verifications. In legal verification, getting four of five results is strictly better than getting zero of five.

Layer three is at the orchestrator level, where asyncio.gather with return exceptions equals true wraps the entire citation verifier and fact checker. If the whole citation verifier agent crashes, the orchestrator catches it and continues with an empty citation results list.

Each layer handles a different failure scope. Layer one handles individual citation failures, which are common because some citations are harder to verify. Layer two handles aggregate failures, which are rare. Layer three handles agent-level failures, which are very rare. Each layer degrades gracefully to the next level of acceptable results. One monolithic try-except could not handle all three scopes without becoming a conditional mess.

Now let's talk about the COULD NOT VERIFY status, which is a design choice that matters deeply in the legal domain. This status exists because legal citation verification has an inherent epistemological limit. A language model cannot authoritatively confirm whether a case exists. Without COULD NOT VERIFY, the model would be forced to choose between SUPPORTED, which is potentially wrong, and NOT SUPPORTED, which is potentially unfair to the attorney. COULD NOT VERIFY is the honest middle ground. In production with a real legal database, items that return COULD NOT VERIFY from the database lookup would genuinely indicate a problem, meaning the case might not exist. From a language model, COULD NOT VERIFY just means "I don't have enough information to make a call."

The status mapping itself is implemented as a dictionary with three entries: supported, not supported, and misleading. COULD NOT VERIFY is intentionally absent from the map. Any unrecognized status the language model returns, including the literal string "could not verify," falls through to the default COULD NOT VERIFY enum value. This is defensive programming against language model output variability. No matter what the model returns, the system produces a valid enum value.

Moving on to the FactCheckerAgent, which is the most expensive agent in the pipeline. This agent reads all four documents side by side, the motion for summary judgment, the police report, the medical records, and the witness statement, and checks whether the lawyer's brief tells the same story as the other documents. If the brief says the accident happened on March 14 but every other document says March 12, that is a contradiction and this agent catches it.

The fact checker makes a single language model call with the most complex prompt in the entire system, 67 lines in the prompts file. The prompt defines eight checking categories. Let me walk through them because knowing these by name signals deep familiarity. The categories are: date consistency, personal protective equipment and safety, work control, scaffolding condition, OSHA compliance, injury details, statute of limitations, and strategic omission. They are ordered by importance, with dates first because dates are the easiest thing to verify objectively, and strategic omission last because it is the most subjective.

Why a single prompt with all eight categories instead of eight separate prompts, one per category? Each separate prompt would make its own language model call, costing roughly eight times the tokens and latency. A single prompt can see all categories simultaneously, which allows it to identify relationships between categories. For example, a wrong date might affect the statute of limitations calculation. The trade-off is prompt complexity. Sixty-seven lines is a lot for one prompt, and the language model must juggle eight simultaneous tasks.

Why these eight specific categories instead of an open-ended instruction like "find any inconsistencies"? Testing showed that the open-ended approach produces wildly variable results. Sometimes it finds the date error, sometimes it does not, sometimes it flags irrelevant differences. Explicit categories act as a checklist that forces the language model to systematically examine each dimension.

The real gem in the fact checker is the set of seven precision rules embedded in the prompt, around lines 46 through 52 of the prompts file. These rules are the product of iterative failure analysis. Each one addresses a specific false-positive pattern that was discovered during development.

For example, one rule says "contradictory means the motion for summary judgment makes a specific claim that is directly refuted." Without this, the language model flagged implicit claims, things like "the motion implies the scaffolding was safe by not mentioning defects," which is not the same as a specific factual claim. Another rule says "an omission is only contradictory if the motion actively hides material evidence." Without this, every fact in the police report that was not mentioned in the motion was flagged as contradictory. But motions for summary judgment are not required to recite every fact from every document. Only material omissions count. A third rule establishes the meta-principle: "when in doubt, prefer consistent or could not verify over contradictory." This encodes the precision-over-recall trade-off. For legal artificial intelligence, false positives, meaning crying wolf, are more damaging than false negatives, meaning missing an error, because false positives erode judicial trust in the system.

These rules live in the prompt rather than in post-processing code because they need to influence the language model's reasoning during generation, not filter its output after the fact. A language model that generates a "contradictory" finding and then has it filtered out has already wasted tokens and processing time. A language model that never generates the spurious finding in the first place is cheaper and faster.

Documents over 6,000 characters are truncated. Why 6,000? This keeps the total prompt under roughly 25,000 tokens across four documents plus instructions, fitting within the context window while leaving room for the response. The truncation is lossy, meaning contradictions in truncated content will be missed, but the alternative, exceeding context limits, would fail entirely.

Now let's talk about the ReportSynthesizerAgent, which is the fourth agent. After the citation verifier and fact checker finish their work in parallel, the synthesizer reads all their results and creates a ranked summary. It takes the JSON-serialized citation and fact verification results, truncated to 10,000 characters each, passes them to the language model with the report synthesis prompt, and produces three outputs: top findings ranked by severity, confidence scores, and a list of unknown issues.

Why a separate synthesis step instead of just concatenating the results from the previous agents? Raw verification results are noisy. The citation verifier might produce ten entries, seven of which say "supported." Those are not findings, they are confirmations. The fact checker might produce entries for all eight categories, five of which say "consistent." A judge does not want to read eighteen entries to find six actual problems. The synthesizer's job is editorial. It surfaces what matters, ranks it by severity, and suppresses what is noise. This is aggregation, not concatenation.

The synthesizer also calculates confidence scores, and an interesting design choice is that the language model does this rather than a formula. Confidence aggregation is a judgment call. Should overall confidence be the mean of all item confidences? The minimum? A weighted average? Each formula embeds assumptions. A language model can consider the full context: three consistent facts and one critical contradiction about a date error that affects the entire case should produce a low overall confidence, but a simple average would produce a misleadingly high number.

The anti-noise instructions in the prompt are also worth mentioning. The prompt explicitly says "do not include mere summaries of consistent or unproblematic facts" and "do not promote could not verify items to findings unless they represent a material gap." These prevent the synthesizer from padding the report with non-findings, which would dilute the signal and make the report harder for a judge to use.

Finally, let's cover the JudicialMemoAgent, the fifth agent and the one that was split off from the synthesizer during development. This agent receives the pre-computed findings and scores, not the raw verification results, and writes a formal memo with four sections: a prose paragraph, a list of key issues, recommended actions, and an overall assessment.

The key interview question here is: why is the memo separate from the synthesizer? The answer comes down to different audiences, different tones, and different prompting strategies. The synthesizer does analytical work. Its prompt says "calculate scores" and "rank by severity." The memo agent does persuasive legal writing. Its prompt says "write in formal legal language" and "be specific about which claims are contradicted and by what evidence." A language model prompted for analysis produces better analysis when it is not simultaneously trying to write polished prose. Similarly, a language model prompted for legal writing produces more fluent and authoritative prose when it is not simultaneously doing arithmetic. Empirical testing confirmed this: splitting the fifth agent out improved both the analytical quality of the synthesizer output and the prose quality of the memo.

The memo agent receives case context so it can reference jurisdiction-specific knowledge when writing. For the Rivera v. Harmon test case, this means the memo can reference "Privette presumption" by name rather than just describing "the misquotation of the cited case."

The structured output choice is worth noting too. The memo returns four fields rather than a single blob of prose. This enables the user interface to display each section separately: the memo text as a paragraph, key issues as distinct items, recommended actions as an action list. If it were a single prose blob, the frontend would need to parse natural language to render it, which is fragile. Structured output means predictable rendering.

One last point about graceful failure across all five agents. Every agent has its own try-except that returns a usable but degraded result on failure. A memo generation failure never prevents the full report from being returned. The report simply has a null or fallback memo. The system degrades through five levels: full report when all agents succeed, report without memo when the memo agent fails, report with partial verification when one of the parallel agents fails, report with only raw data when the synthesizer fails, and error report when the parser fails. The parser failure is the only immediately fatal one because without citations, the citation verifier has nothing to verify.


Chapter Five: Citation Verification, the Key Topic

This is the topic the interview panel will push on hardest, so let's be completely honest about the current state, articulate why it matters, and lay out the production vision. The goal is not to hide the gap but to demonstrate that you understand its implications and have a credible plan.

Here is the current state, stated plainly. Citation verification in the submitted system relies on the language model's parametric knowledge. That means when the system checks whether a legal case exists or whether its holding was accurately described, it is asking the language model "do you remember this case from your training data?" For the known test case, Rivera v. Harmon, the system gets enriched context through a file called case context dot py. This is a 19-line file. Let me say that again: 19 lines. It contains a single dictionary that maps exactly one case identifier to a block of handwritten legal knowledge about the Privette presumption, the Seabright decision, and the jurisdictional status of out-of-state citations. For any case identifier that is not Rivera v. Harmon, the function returns an empty string, and the language model receives zero domain-specific guidance.

The honest assessment for the interview goes like this: "For the submission, citation verification relies on the language model's training data for case existence and holding accuracy. This is the biggest gap for production."

Now let's talk about why this gap matters, and let's not be abstract about it. In 2023, in the case Mata v. Avianca, a lawyer submitted a brief to a federal court that cited six cases generated by ChatGPT. None of them existed. The judge sanctioned the lawyer and the firm five thousand dollars. That might sound like a slap on the wrist, but the reputational damage was severe, and the case became the founding example of judicial distrust toward artificial intelligence. Since then, the consequences have escalated. In Crabill, a lawyer received a one-year suspension. In Park v. Kim at the Second Circuit, the case was referred to a grievance panel. Courts are becoming less tolerant, not more tolerant, of AI-generated errors. The window for "I didn't know ChatGPT could hallucinate" has closed.

A fabricated citation is worse than a null pointer exception. It can result in sanctions, case dismissal, and loss of a bar license. For a tool that is meant to verify briefs on behalf of courts, relying on parametric memory for citation accuracy is fundamentally insufficient.

This is why Learned Hand, the company this work targets, built what they call a "closed universe." The answer to hallucination is not better prompts. It is constrained retrieval. The system should only be able to reference documents that exist in a curated, verified corpus. If a case is not in the corpus, it cannot be cited and should not be affirmed. The system should return COULD NOT VERIFY, honestly, rather than fabricating confidence.

Let's walk through the case context file to understand the current implementation and why the architecture is actually sound even though the backend is a placeholder. The file lives at backend utils case context dot py. It defines a constant called RIVERA V HARMON CONTEXT, which is a multi-line string containing three pieces of legal knowledge: first, that Privette v. Superior Court established a presumption against hirer liability, not absolute immunity, and the word "never" does not appear in the holding; second, that Seabright Insurance Co. v. US Airways actually narrowed the retained control exception; and third, that this is a California state court case and out-of-state citations are non-binding persuasive authority only. Below this constant is a function called get case context that performs a simple dictionary lookup. One entry. Unknown case identifiers return an empty string.

The context string flows through the pipeline as a prompt fragment. The orchestrator loads it and passes it to the citation verifier, the fact checker, and the judicial memo agent. In the citation verification prompt, there is a placeholder called case context that receives either the full Rivera context or an empty string. When it is populated, the language model has specific knowledge to catch errors like the insertion of the word "never" into the Privette holding. When it is empty, the language model falls back entirely on what it remembers from training, which may include the same mischaracterization.

Here is what to tell the interviewer about the case context approach: the pattern is architecturally correct but the implementation is a placeholder. In production, get case context would query a legal database instead of returning a hardcoded string. The interface is right. The case context variable in the prompt is the correct injection point. The backend just needs to be replaced.

Now let's lay out the five-tier production citation verification model, because having a concrete production vision is what makes the gap seem like a design decision rather than an oversight.

Tier one is existence verification. Query a case law database, verify the volume, reporter, and page number, confirm the case name matches. This is a deterministic lookup with no language model judgment needed. CourtListener would be the first integration target because it is free, open-source, and covers all federal cases. Westlaw or LexisNexis would provide comprehensive state coverage based on business needs.

Tier two is holding accuracy. Use retrieval-augmented generation to retrieve the actual opinion text, then compare the brief's characterization against the real holding. The language model's role shifts from "do you know this case?" which is unreliable, to "does this retrieved passage support the brief's claim?" which is much more reliable because the language model is reading the actual text rather than recalling from memory.

Tier three is jurisdictional authority. Parse the reporter to determine jurisdiction. South Western Third Series means Texas. Southern Third Series means Florida. California Fourth means California. This is a rule-based lookup table with no language model needed. Flag non-binding authority automatically.

Tier four is subsequent treatment, which is the legal process known as Shepardizing or KeyCite checking. Is the case overruled, reversed, questioned, or distinguished? A citation to an overruled case is the most dangerous kind of error because it is a real case the attorney may have found through legitimate research, but the law has changed since it was decided.

Tier five is proposition-level verification. Extract the specific legal question the brief claims the case answers, then search the opinion for relevant passages. This catches the subtlest error: a real case, correctly cited, still in good standing, but mischaracterized. The brief says the case holds one thing when it actually holds something different or more nuanced. This is where legal domain expertise in the prompt matters most.

The current system has the logic for tiers one through three in the citation verifier, but backed by language model knowledge instead of a database. The architecture does not change. Only the knowledge source does. The case context injection pattern is the interface through which database results would flow.

This connects directly to Learned Hand's design philosophy. Courts work with closed document universes, meaning the case record. A judge evaluating a motion for summary judgment works with the filed documents, the cited authorities, and existing case law. This is not an open-ended web search. It is a constrained retrieval problem, which is precisely what retrieval-augmented generation excels at. The closed universe approach eliminates the fabrication vector entirely. If a case is not in the corpus, the system cannot cite it and should not confirm it.

The COULD NOT VERIFY status partially addresses this gap in the current system. It exists as a safe escape hatch so the language model does not have to choose between affirming something it is unsure about and condemning something that might be correct. In theory, the language model should use this status when uncertain. In practice, language models are overconfident. They tend to assert either SUPPORTED or NOT SUPPORTED rather than admitting uncertainty. With a real legal database behind the system, items that return COULD NOT VERIFY would carry much more meaning: the case genuinely could not be found, which is a strong signal of potential fabrication.

Here is the precise framing for the interview: "The verification logic is architecturally sound. The knowledge backend needs to be replaced with authoritative legal databases. The interface is designed for that swap. The case context variable in every agent's prompt is the injection point. In production, you replace the 19-line lookup function with a database query, and the downstream logic remains identical."

Let me add one more point about the COULD NOT VERIFY design choice that shows depth of understanding. The status map in the citation verifier has three entries: supported, not supported, and misleading. COULD NOT VERIFY is intentionally absent from the map. Any unrecognized string the language model returns falls through to the COULD NOT VERIFY default. This means the system is biased toward honesty over false confidence. An unexpected output from the model does not get silently categorized as supported or not supported. It lands in the "I do not know" bucket, which is the safest place for it to land in a legal context.

When you discuss this in the interview, connect it to the broader principle. In legal artificial intelligence, the cost of a false positive, crying wolf about a citation that is actually valid, and the cost of a false negative, missing a fabricated citation, are both high, but in different ways. False positives erode judicial trust in the tool. False negatives risk real harm to the legal process. The precision-over-recall trade-off encoded throughout the system, from the fact checker's precision rules to the COULD NOT VERIFY default, reflects a deliberate choice to prioritize trust. A system that judges learn to trust, even if it misses some errors, is more valuable than a system that catches everything but also generates false alarms that waste judicial time.


Chapter Six: The Eval Harness, Your Standout

Vienna called the evaluation harness "the standout." That is not a throwaway compliment. In a field where most take-home submissions are evaluated on whether they work at all, having a comprehensive, multi-layered evaluation framework signals engineering maturity. Let's go deep on every layer so you can talk about this with authority and enthusiasm.

The eval harness uses a five-layer evaluation approach. Layer one is keyword matching. Layer two is LLM-as-judge semantic evaluation. Layer three is the combined scoring that merges keyword and semantic results. Layer four is the comprehensive evaluation suite with 31 assertions across six categories. Layer five is SQLite persistence keyed by git commit SHA for tracking quality over time. Let's walk through each one.

Layer one, keyword matching, lives in the metrics file. The core function is called finding matches discrepancy. It takes a pipeline finding and a ground truth discrepancy, concatenates all searchable text from the finding including description, type, and evidence, normalizes everything to lowercase, and checks whether at least two keywords from the ground truth appear in the text. That threshold of two is important. Requiring at least two keyword matches prevents false positives from incidental word overlap. If you changed that threshold from two to one, false match rates would spike dramatically.

Layer two is the LLM-as-judge, which lives in a separate file and is activated by setting an environment variable called LLM JUDGE to one. The judge receives a structured comparison: here is the ground truth discrepancy with its ID, description, category, expected evidence, and expected reasoning; here is the pipeline finding with its description, type, evidence, and confidence. The judge evaluates three criteria: Is this the same factual or legal issue? Does it cite similar evidence? Would a legal professional agree these match? The judge returns a JSON response with a match boolean, a confidence score between zero and one, and reasoning. A match requires both a true match value and a confidence of at least 0.5. The judge runs at temperature zero for deterministic output.

Now here is the question you should be prepared to answer: why use both keyword matching and LLM-as-judge? Why not just one? Keyword matching is deterministic, fast, and transparent. You can look at the keywords and the finding text and understand exactly why something matched or did not. But it is brittle. If the pipeline describes a "date discrepancy" but the ground truth keywords expect "incorrect incident date," the keyword matcher misses it. The LLM-as-judge catches semantic equivalences that keyword matching misses. It understands that "date discrepancy" and "incorrect incident date" describe the same error. But the LLM judge is non-deterministic by nature, even at temperature zero across different providers or model versions. It adds language model uncertainty into the measurement of language model accuracy, which is epistemologically problematic.

Together they complement each other. The combined metrics layer, layer three, takes the union of keyword matches and LLM judge matches. A discrepancy is considered detected if either method found it. The output includes diagnostic breakdowns: matches found by keywords only, matches found by the LLM judge only, matches agreed upon by both, and items missed by both. This union strategy compensates for each method's false negatives while giving you transparency into where they agree and disagree.

Layer four is the comprehensive evaluation suite, and this is where the depth really shows. The system runs 31 assertions across six categories. Let me walk through each category.

The first category is recall, with eight checks. The test document for recall is Rivera v. Harmon, which has eight deliberately planted errors. Each error has an identifier from D-01 through D-08, a category, and a weight. The four critical errors carry double weight: D-01 is a date inconsistency where the motion says March 14 but every other document says March 12; D-02 is a personal protective equipment contradiction where the motion claims no PPE was used but the evidence shows a harness, hard hat, and safety vest; D-03 is a citation misquotation where the word "never" was inserted into the Privette holding, changing a rebuttable presumption into absolute immunity; and D-05 is a work control contradiction where the motion claims no control but the police report documents that Foreman Donner directed the work. The four medium-weight errors carry single weight: D-04 is using the wrong incident date in the statute of limitations calculation; D-06 is citing Texas and Florida cases as binding authority in a California court; D-07 is omitting the rust and plywood defects from the scaffolding condition; and D-08 is failing to disclose a post-incident scaffolding rebuild that could constitute spoliation of evidence. The total possible weight is twelve. Catching all four critical items but missing all four medium items gives a recall of eight out of twelve, or sixty-seven percent.

The weighted recall formula is straightforward: sum the weights of items that passed, divide by the sum of all weights. This reflects legal impact. A missed date error that affects the statute of limitations calculation is more consequential than a missed note about scaffolding condition, and the weighting scheme captures that.

The ground truth matching uses two modes. In "any" mode, used for items like the date error and scaffolding condition, any single keyword found in the concatenated text triggers a match. This works when a keyword is sufficiently specific, because finding "march 14" in the output strongly implies the date discrepancy was detected. In "keyword plus signal" mode, used for items like PPE and Privette misquotation, the system requires both a keyword and a signal word. For the PPE check, merely mentioning "harness" is not enough. The pipeline must also indicate it is a contradiction, using words like "contradict" or "inconsistent." This prevents false matches where the system mentions safety equipment descriptively but fails to flag the actual inconsistency.

The second category is precision, with four checks. This is where the clean document test case comes in. Smith v. ABC Corp is a set of four documents, motion, police report, medical records, and witness statement, that are completely internally consistent. All documents agree on the date, all confirm protective equipment was worn, no safety violations, no contradictions, no planted errors. The precision tests expect at most one finding, ideally zero, zero contradictory facts, zero citations flagged as not supported or misleading, and overall confidence of at least 0.6. The threshold of "at most one" rather than exactly zero accommodates language model noise without masking systematic false positive patterns. If the system consistently produces three or four findings on clean documents, that is a precision problem the eval catches.

The third category is hallucination detection, with four checks. H-01 verifies no fabricated case names appear by checking six hardcoded fake names against the verified citations. H-02 verifies that citations reference real known cases, requiring at least two of eight known case names like Privette, Seabright, Dixon, and Okafor to appear. H-03 is the grounding check, which I will discuss separately in a moment. H-04 checks for invented document types by looking for references to depositions, expert reports, surveillance, or tax returns, none of which exist in the test documents.

The fourth category is cross-document consistency, with five checks. These verify that the pipeline actually cross-references multiple documents: at least one fact cites contradictory or supporting sources across documents, the pipeline references the police report, the pipeline references the witness statement, the pipeline references the medical records, and the metadata confirms all four document types were analyzed.

The fifth category is uncertainty expression, with four checks. These verify that the system does not claim false certainty. At least one citation should have confidence below 1.0. Confidence scores should show appropriate spread, not all 1.0 and not all 0.0. An unknown issues list should exist. And no citation flagged as not supported or misleading should claim one hundred percent confidence, because that would be the system claiming absolute certainty about a judgment that is inherently probabilistic.

The sixth category is structural validation, with six checks. These verify the report has required fields, citations and facts are structured objects not raw text, confidence scores are valid floats between zero and one, the judicial memo is a structured dictionary with a memo field longer than fifty characters, and finding IDs are present and unique.

Now let's come back to the grounding check, which deserves its own discussion because it addresses one of the most critical risks in legal AI: fabricated evidence. The grounding function lives in the metrics file and verifies that each finding's evidence text actually appears in the source documents. It does this through substring matching with a minimum length of ten characters. Why ten characters? Shorter substrings produce false matches. The word "March" at five characters appears in many contexts and is not specific enough. But "March 12, 2021" at fourteen characters is specific enough to confirm the finding references actual document text.

The grounding check has two tiers of matching. The primary check looks for the full normalized evidence string as a substring in the combined source text, with a minimum length of ten characters. The fallback splits the evidence on period delimiters and checks individual sentence fragments, each requiring at least fifteen characters. The grounding rate equals the number of grounded findings divided by total findings. Ungrounded items are tracked with their index and a truncated description for debugging.

This matters enormously for legal AI. A system that generates findings based on text that does not exist in the source documents is hallucinating. A grounding check that confirms at least fifty percent of findings reference actual source text is a minimum threshold for trustworthiness. In production you would want this much higher.

Now for Layer five, persistence. Every eval run is stored in a SQLite database using Write-Ahead Logging mode, which enables concurrent reads during writes. The schema tracks the run identifier, timestamp, git SHA, precision, recall, F1 score, hallucination rate, true positives, false positives, false negatives, and three JSON blob columns for full metrics, findings, and the complete pipeline report. The git SHA is captured by running git rev-parse short HEAD with a three-second timeout to prevent hangs in non-git environments. This links each eval run to the exact code commit, enabling regression tracking across deploys. The retrieval function returns the most recent runs ordered by timestamp, with JSON columns deserialized back to Python objects.

The git SHA tracking is particularly valuable because it lets you answer the question "did this commit make things better or worse?" If you change a prompt and recall drops from seventy-five percent to sixty percent, you can see that immediately in the eval database and correlate it with the specific code change. This is the kind of engineering rigor that separates prototype work from production-ready systems.

Let's talk about how to discuss scaling the eval and preventing overfitting, because these are likely follow-up questions. The current eval uses a single test case with planted errors, Rivera v. Harmon, and a single clean case, Smith v. ABC Corp. That is sufficient for a take-home submission but insufficient for production. To scale, you would build a corpus of real motion for summary judgment filings with annotated errors. Each case type exercises different fact categories. A contract dispute case would test different categories than a personal injury case. An automated regression suite would run across the full corpus on every commit.

To prevent overfitting, the clean document precision test already acts as a negative control. If you over-tune prompts to catch the eight planted errors, you risk increasing false positives on clean documents, and the precision tests will catch that. Beyond that, you would add adversarial cases with subtle errors and near-misses, periodically refresh the test corpus so the prompts cannot be tuned to a static set, and use held-out test cases that are never used during prompt development.

There is also the promptfoo integration worth mentioning. Promptfoo configuration files enable A/B testing of prompt variants side by side, quantifying the impact of prompt engineering changes. The precise versus imprecise prompt comparison measures exactly how much the precision rules in the fact checking prompt contribute to reducing false positives. This is measurement-driven prompt engineering rather than guesswork.

When you discuss the eval in the interview, the framing that resonates is this: "I built the eval harness before tuning prompts. Thirty-one assertions, weighted recall, grounding checks. Without measurement, prompt engineering is guesswork. The eval is the most deterministic component in the system because the eval itself should not be a source of uncertainty." That framing, measurement before optimization, signals the kind of engineering discipline that transfers from a take-home submission to production systems. And the fact that Vienna specifically called it out as the standout means it landed exactly as intended.


Chapter Seven: Trade-offs and Design Decisions

Let us talk about the decisions that shaped this system, because in any interview about a technical project, the most revealing answers are not about what you built but about why you built it that way, and what you chose not to do. Every design decision in the BS Detector reflects a deliberate trade-off, and understanding those trade-offs is what separates someone who assembled code from someone who engineered a system.

The first and most important trade-off is precision over recall. In most machine learning contexts, recall is king. You want to catch every spam email, every fraudulent transaction, every anomaly. Missing one is costly. But legal AI inverts this completely. In a courtroom setting, a false positive, meaning the system flags something as an error when it is actually correct, is more damaging than a false negative, meaning the system misses an actual error. Here is why. When a judge or law clerk sees the system cry wolf, flagging a perfectly legitimate citation or marking a consistent fact as contradictory, trust evaporates immediately. Judges are already skeptical of AI after the Mata v. Avianca debacle where attorneys submitted fabricated citations. One false alarm in your report, and the entire output gets dismissed. The judge stops reading. Contrast that with a false negative: the system misses an error, but the brief still goes through normal human review. The error might be caught downstream. It is not ideal, but it does not destroy the tool's credibility. This asymmetry is encoded directly into the fact-checking prompt through seven explicit precision rules. Rule six is the meta-rule, the one that captures the entire philosophy: "When in doubt, prefer consistent or could not verify over contradictory." Every other rule flows from this principle. Rule one requires a specific claim that is directly refuted before marking something contradictory. Rule two says an omission is only contradictory if the brief actively hides material evidence, not just because it fails to mention a routine fact. Rule seven tells the model to skip categories that do not apply to the case, preventing it from generating scaffolding condition findings in a contract dispute. These rules exist because each one was added to fix a specific false positive pattern observed during development. The prompt is precisely as long as it needs to be. Removing any rule reintroduces the failure it was designed to prevent.

Now let us talk about the single LLM call per agent design. Each agent in the pipeline makes exactly one call to the language model with a carefully crafted prompt. There is no multi-turn conversation, no iterative refinement, no back-and-forth. This might seem like a limitation, and in some ways it is, but it was the right call for several reasons. First, simplicity. A single call with a structured prompt is easier to reason about, easier to debug, and easier to evaluate than a multi-turn dialogue where the model's second response depends on its first. Second, speed and cost. Each additional LLM call adds three to ten seconds of latency and costs tokens. Five agents making one call each is five calls total. Five agents making three calls each is fifteen calls, tripling both time and cost. Third, predictability. When the model produces its output in a single pass, you can evaluate that output deterministically. When the model refines its answer across multiple turns, the final output depends on the path it took, which varies from run to run. Fourth, the results are good enough. The evaluation harness shows the pipeline catches six to eight of eight planted errors with single-pass prompts. Multi-turn refinement would be justified in production for low-confidence findings, items where the model returns a confidence score of point three or below. The architecture supports adding a refinement loop. You would wrap the verifier in a while loop that re-analyzes items below a confidence threshold with additional context or retrieval augmentation. But for the take-home, single pass was the Pareto-optimal choice.

The third major trade-off is schema injection versus native structured output. The system includes the expected JSON schema as text within the prompt rather than using OpenAI's native response format parameter or function calling. This is a portability decision. DeepSeek's OpenAI-compatible API has inconsistent support for the response format parameter and function calling. Ollama's support varies by model. But every model, regardless of provider, can read a JSON schema embedded in a prompt and attempt to produce output matching that schema. Schema injection through LangChain works across DeepSeek, Ollama, and any future model provider. The cost is reliability. Native structured output guarantees valid JSON. Schema injection requires a three-layer parsing pipeline in the LLM service: first, try to parse the response as raw JSON. Second, strip markdown code fences and try again. Third, look for nested brackets and extract the JSON object. This parsing pipeline handles the messiness of real LLM output, but it is more fragile than native structured output would be. The trade-off is clear: portability across models versus parsing reliability with a single model. For a system designed to be model-agnostic, portability wins.

The fourth decision is the choice of DeepSeek and Ollama over frontier models like GPT-4 or Claude. DeepSeek was actually an assignment constraint, but it turned out to be architecturally instructive. The system abstracts the model behind an LLM service layer. Swapping to a different model requires changing an environment variable, not rewriting pipeline code. Ollama provides local development capability with no API key required, which is valuable for rapid iteration and for environments where sending legal documents to a cloud API raises confidentiality concerns. The important caveat, and this matters for anyone who works with LLMs, is that prompt engineering is model-specific. The seven precision rules in the fact-checking prompt were tuned for DeepSeek's behavior. A different model might have different false-positive patterns, different default confidence distributions, different tendencies around verbosity or conservatism. Changing models requires re-running the evaluation suite and potentially re-tuning prompts. The architecture is model-agnostic. The prompts are not.

The fifth trade-off is the graceful degradation hierarchy, and this is where the system's philosophy really shows. The pipeline defines five levels of degradation, from full success down to pipeline failure, and each level provides the maximum useful output possible. Level zero is full success: all five agents complete, and you get a complete verification report with citations, facts, findings, confidence scores, and a judicial memo. Level one is a report without a memo: the memo agent failed, but you still have everything else, all the citations, all the facts, all the findings and confidence scores. Level two is partial verification: one of the two parallel agents, either the citation verifier or the fact checker, failed, but the other completed successfully. You get results from the healthy agent with a clear status indicator showing what failed. Level three is raw data only: the synthesizer failed, so you have verified citations and verified facts but no ranked findings or aggregate confidence scores. Level four is an error report: the parser failed, and without parsed citations, the citation verifier has nothing to verify. This is the only truly fatal failure. The parser failure is fatal because running only the fact checker would produce a misleadingly partial result. A user seeing fact-checking results with no citation results might assume citations were clean when they were never checked at all. Better to clearly report the failure than to produce an incomplete analysis that looks complete.

Let me close this chapter by talking about the sixty-seven-line prompt, because this encapsulates something fundamental about LLM applications that many engineers miss. In a traditional software system, business logic lives in code. Functions, conditionals, loops, data transformations. In an LLM application, prompts are business logic. The fact checker's sixty-seven-line prompt is not a user message to the model. It is a specification. It defines eight checking categories ordered by importance, from date consistency at the top to strategic omission at the bottom. It includes seven precision rules that encode the precision-over-recall trade-off. It specifies the output format through an embedded JSON schema. It tells the model which document is being verified, the motion for summary judgment, versus which documents are evidence sources. Every line was added to fix a specific failure mode. The prompt is the business logic. If you change the prompt, you change what the system does. If you remove a precision rule, you reintroduce the false positive it was designed to prevent. Treating prompts as configuration rather than code is a mistake. They deserve the same rigor, version control, and evaluation discipline as any production code.


Chapter Eight: Production Vision

Now let us shift gears and talk about what would need to change if this system were deployed in an actual courthouse. The architecture is production-ready in a specific sense: the five-agent decomposition with typed contracts, parallel execution, graceful degradation, and deterministic orchestration is sound. What needs to change is not how the components communicate but what each component does internally. Let me walk through the major production enhancements, starting with the most critical.

The single most important production change is legal database integration for citation verification. Right now, the citation verifier relies on the LLM's parametric knowledge, its training data, to determine whether a case exists and whether it says what the brief claims. This is fundamentally unreliable. An LLM does not actually know whether a case exists. It predicts whether the citation looks plausible based on patterns in its training data. It cannot distinguish between a real case it has seen and a fabricated case that sounds plausible. In production, you would integrate with legal databases at multiple tiers. The first tier is CourtListener, which is a free, open-source database covering all federal cases with a well-documented API. You would use it for existence verification: query the database with the case name and citation, confirm the volume, reporter, and page number match, and verify the case name is correct. This is a deterministic lookup with no LLM judgment required. The second tier is Westlaw or LexisNexis for comprehensive state coverage, depending on business needs and licensing arrangements. The third tier is retrieval-augmented generation over the actual case text. Once you have confirmed a case exists and retrieved the opinion, the LLM's role shifts fundamentally. Instead of asking "do you know this case," you ask "does this retrieved passage support the brief's claim." This is a much more constrained and reliable task because the LLM is reading actual text, not recalling from memory. The fourth tier is treatment checking through Shepard's Citations or KeyCite, verifying whether a case has been overruled, reversed, questioned, or distinguished by subsequent decisions. A citation to an overruled case is the most dangerous kind of error because the case is real, the citation format is correct, and the holding was accurately stated at one time, but the law has changed. The fifth tier is proposition-level verification: extracting the specific legal question the brief claims a case answers and searching the opinion for relevant passages. This catches the subtlest error of all, a real case, correctly cited, but subtly mischaracterized. The current system has the logic for the first three tiers in the citation verifier, but backed by LLM knowledge instead of a database. The case context injection pattern, the curly brace placeholder in the prompt, is exactly the interface through which database results would flow. The architecture does not change. Only the knowledge source changes.

The second critical production need is document processing. The current system accepts plain text input. Real legal filings come as PDFs, and many are scanned documents requiring optical character recognition, or OCR. A production document pipeline would use libraries like PyPDF2 or pdfplumber for digitally-created PDFs where text can be extracted directly, and Tesseract OCR for scanned documents where the text exists only as images. Beyond extraction, you need document type classification. A motion for summary judgment filing typically includes the memorandum of points and authorities, a separate statement of undisputed facts, declarations, and exhibits. Each document type needs different processing. The memorandum needs citation extraction. The separate statement needs fact extraction organized by numbered fact. Exhibits need to be linked to the specific facts they support. This classification step would be a new agent or a preprocessing layer before the existing pipeline.

The third major production enhancement is streaming via Server-Sent Events, commonly abbreviated as SSE. The current system uses a synchronous request-response pattern. The client sends documents and waits thirty to sixty seconds for the complete report. For a take-home demonstration, this is tolerable. In production, where a complex brief with many citations might take three to five minutes to process, the user needs real-time progress updates. SSE is the right transport for this, not WebSocket. SSE is simpler because it is HTTP-based with automatic reconnection and requires no handshake. It is sufficient because the communication is one-directional, server to client. The client does not need to send messages during pipeline execution. And SSE works through proxies and load balancers without special configuration, which matters in government IT environments where network infrastructure is often restrictive. The streaming architecture would emit events as each pipeline stage completes: parser started, parser complete with citation count, verification started, each individual citation verified as it completes, fact checking started, fact checking complete, synthesis started, and finally pipeline complete with the full report. This gives law clerks a sense of progress and lets them start reviewing citation results even before fact checking finishes.

The fourth production enhancement is confidence calibration. Right now, confidence scores are raw LLM estimates. When the model says zero point eight five, it does not mean that eighty-five percent of items with this score are correct. It means the model feels eighty-five percent sure, which is a fundamentally different statement. LLM confidence scores suffer from three problems: overconfidence, where models tend to assign high confidence even when uncertain; arbitrary precision, where a score of zero point eight seven implies a level of precision that does not exist; and context sensitivity, where the same finding might get different confidence scores depending on what other findings are in the same prompt. Production calibration requires collecting data across many cases with known ground truth, then applying statistical methods to map raw scores to actual probabilities. Platt scaling fits a logistic regression curve to the relationship between raw confidence and actual accuracy. Isotonic regression is a non-parametric alternative that is more flexible but requires more data. For judicial consumption, the most practical approach might be discrete confidence bands rather than continuous scores. High confidence, meaning above zero point eight, would indicate the system is very confident in this finding. Medium confidence, between zero point five and zero point eight, means the finding is likely but warrants review. Low confidence, between zero point two and zero point five, means the finding is uncertain and requires manual verification. Unverifiable, below zero point two, means the system cannot make a determination. These bands are more honest and more useful than a spuriously precise zero point eight seven. Beyond calibration, the system should differentiate between verified confidence, meaning this was checked against a database, and estimated confidence, meaning this is an AI assessment. A judge needs to know whether the confidence comes from a deterministic database lookup or a probabilistic model judgment. The source of confidence matters more than the number.

The fifth area is hallucination mitigation, and this is where the production system needs defense in depth. The BS Detector already implements some layers, but production requires all six. Layer one is the closed universe principle: verification happens only against curated, verified sources. If a case is not in the corpus, the system returns "could not verify" rather than fabricating an answer. This aligns directly with Learned Hand's architecture. Layer two is retrieval-augmented generation: retrieve actual case opinions from the legal database and verify quotes against the source text, never against the LLM's memory. Layer three is multi-agent cross-validation: the citation verifier and fact checker are independent agents analyzing different aspects of the same documents. Disagreements between them surface as flags, not silent failures. Layer four is structured output constraints: Pydantic enums force the output into a constrained space. The verification status enum has exactly four values, supported, not supported, could not verify, and misleading. The model cannot return "probably fine" or "maybe wrong." Layer five is the confidence calibration just discussed. Layer six is human-in-the-loop review: low-confidence items require explicit human sign-off before they appear in the final report. Side-by-side source links let the reviewer verify every claim against the original document.

Let me also address the production readiness assessment more broadly. There are three tiers of priority. The critical tier includes legal database integration, PDF and document processing, and authentication and security. Without these, the system cannot be deployed. The medium priority tier includes caching frequently cited cases for performance, monitoring and observability through distributed tracing with tools like Langfuse or OpenTelemetry, confidence calibration, and expanding the evaluation corpus from one test case to fifty or more annotated briefs across different practice areas. The lower priority tier includes multi-jurisdiction support where the system dynamically adjusts its analysis based on filing jurisdiction, batch processing for overnight queue processing of court dockets, and advanced features like multi-turn iterative refinement for low-confidence findings. The key insight is that the architecture is already production-ready even though the system is not. Production readiness has two orthogonal dimensions: architecture, meaning how components communicate, and implementation, meaning what each component does internally. Adding legal database integration means changing what the citation verifier does internally, not how it connects to the rest of the pipeline. The one architectural change that would be needed at scale is in the fact checker. With fifty-page documents, the six-thousand-character truncation would lose approximately eighty percent of the content. Fixing this requires either chunking within the fact checker or adding a document summarization agent. That is the only true architectural change needed for production.


Chapter Nine: Legal Domain Knowledge

Let us now turn to the legal domain itself, because building a system that checks legal documents requires understanding what those documents actually do and how courts actually work. You do not need to be a lawyer, but you do need to speak the language accurately, and you need to understand why certain errors matter more than others.

Let me start with the Privette presumption, because it is central to the test case used in the BS Detector. In California workers' compensation and tort law, the Privette presumption says that when a company hires an independent contractor to do a job, the hiring company is generally not liable for workplace injuries suffered by the contractor's employees. The logic is straightforward: the independent contractor controls its own work, provides its own safety equipment, and manages its own employees. The hirer should not be responsible for risks created by the contractor's own operations. But here is the critical detail that the BS Detector catches: Privette is a presumption, not an absolute rule. It is rebuttable. There are exceptions, most notably the retained control exception. If the hirer actually directed or controlled the contractor's work, the presumption does not apply. In the Rivera v. Harmon test case, the brief claims that under Privette, defendants are "never liable." But Privette does not say "never." The doctrine has well-established exceptions, and the word "never" is a misquotation that strengthens the brief's argument beyond what the law actually supports. This is a mischaracterization, not a fabrication. The case is real. The citation is correct. But the brief describes the holding inaccurately. This is one of the most dangerous types of legal error because it looks legitimate on its face and requires domain knowledge to catch.

Now let us talk about binding versus persuasive authority, because this is fundamental to how the legal system works. Not all cases carry the same weight. Binding authority, also called mandatory authority, must be followed. If the California Supreme Court decides that a statute means a certain thing, every court in California must apply that interpretation. A lower court that ignores binding authority will be reversed on appeal. Persuasive authority is different. It can be considered, it can be cited, it can be influential, but it does not have to be followed. A Texas appellate court's interpretation of workers' compensation law might be interesting to a California judge, but the California judge is not bound by it. The BS Detector catches exactly this issue. In the Rivera test case, the brief cites Dixon v. Summit Builders, a Texas case, and Okafor v. Pacific Construction, a Florida case, in a California motion for summary judgment. These citations are technically valid. The cases exist. They may even say what the brief claims. But they are persuasive authority at best, not binding. They cannot override California binding precedent like Privette v. Superior Court. A production citation verification system would need a jurisdiction hierarchy engine that automatically classifies each cited case as binding or persuasive based on the filing court and the cited court's jurisdiction. The reporter abbreviation often tells you the jurisdiction: S.W.3d is Texas, So.3d is Florida, Cal.4th is the California Supreme Court, Cal.App.5th is a California Court of Appeal. This parsing can be rule-based, no LLM needed, just a lookup table mapping reporter abbreviations to jurisdictions.

Let me explain spoliation, because it comes up in the test case and it is a concept that non-lawyers often misunderstand. Spoliation is the destruction or significant alteration of evidence that may be relevant to litigation. It is not just carelessness. In legal contexts, spoliation can lead to an adverse inference, meaning the court may instruct the jury to assume that the destroyed evidence was unfavorable to the party that destroyed it. In the Rivera test case, the scaffolding involved in the worker's injury was rebuilt after the incident. This post-incident reconstruction destroyed the physical evidence of the scaffolding's condition, including the rust and plywood defects documented in other records. The brief's failure to mention this reconstruction is flagged by the BS Detector as a strategic omission. The distinction between cleanup and spoliation matters: routine cleanup of a worksite is not spoliation. But reconstructing the specific piece of equipment involved in an injury, thereby making it impossible to inspect the original condition, raises serious spoliation concerns, especially if litigation was foreseeable at the time of reconstruction.

Now let us discuss motions for summary judgment, or MSJs, because this is the document type the BS Detector is built to analyze. A motion for summary judgment is filed when one party believes there is no genuine dispute of material fact and that they are entitled to judgment as a matter of law. In plain language, the filing party is arguing that even looking at all the evidence in the best possible light for the other side, no reasonable jury could find against them. There is nothing to try. The case should be decided by the judge, right now, without a trial. The MSJ lifecycle follows a specific pattern. The moving party files the motion, which includes a memorandum of points and authorities laying out the legal argument with case citations, a separate statement of undisputed facts with citations to evidence in the record, and exhibits containing the actual evidence. The opposing party then files an opposition following the same structure, disputing facts and citing counter-evidence. The moving party may file a reply. Then there is typically a hearing, followed by the judge's ruling. A huge percentage of civil cases are resolved at the MSJ stage, which is precisely why MSJ verification is such a high-value use case for AI tools.

Understanding who actually reads these motions matters for product design. In practice, the judge rarely reads an MSJ cold. A law clerk or staff attorney reads it first and writes a bench memo, an internal document summarizing the arguments, evaluating the strength of each side's position, flagging problems, and recommending a tentative ruling. The judge reads the bench memo, reviews key portions of the briefing, and makes a decision. Law clerks are the primary consumers of tools like the BS Detector and like Learned Hand's product. They need comprehensive analysis they can verify quickly. They need the tool to flag problems so they know where to focus their own careful reading. They do not need the tool to make decisions. The tool should augment the clerk's analysis, never replace it, because every litigant has a constitutional right to have their case actually read and considered by a human judge.

Let us talk about citation systems, because understanding how legal citations work is essential for building a verification system. Legal citations follow a standardized format defined by the Bluebook, which is formally titled The Bluebook: A Uniform System of Citation. The basic structure for a case citation includes the case name, then a volume number, then the reporter abbreviation, then a page number, then the court and year in parentheses. For example, Privette v. Superior Court, volume five, California Reports Fourth Series, page six hundred eighty-nine, decided in nineteen ninety-three. The reporter tells you which court decided the case. Federal reporters include United States Reports, abbreviated U.S., for Supreme Court decisions, the Federal Reporter in its second, third, and fourth series for Circuit Court decisions, and Federal Supplement for District Court decisions. Each state has its own reporter series. Pinpoint citations add a specific page after the starting page, indicating where exactly in the opinion the cited proposition appears. This matters for verification because the system needs to check not just that the case exists but that the specific page cited actually contains the claimed holding. Parallel citations reference the same case across multiple reporter volumes. The same California Supreme Court decision might appear in California Reports, California Reporter, and Pacific Reporter. A citation verification system needs to understand that these are one case, not three.

Shepardizing is a term every legal professional knows, and it refers to the process of checking whether a cited case is still good law. The name comes from Shepard's Citations, a service now owned by LexisNexis. Westlaw's equivalent is called KeyCite. When you Shepardize a case, you are checking whether subsequent court decisions have overruled it, reversed it, questioned it, distinguished it, or affirmed it. A citation can be technically correct in every way, the case exists, the citation format is accurate, the holding is properly characterized, but legally useless because the case was overruled five years later. This is the next level of citation verification beyond existence checking, and in a production system, it would be implemented as the fourth tier of the five-tier citation verification model.

Finally, let me explain why post-conviction relief matters so much for Learned Hand specifically. After a defendant is convicted in a criminal case and has exhausted their direct appeals, they can file for post-conviction relief, typically through a habeas corpus petition. These petitions argue that the conviction was constitutionally flawed due to ineffective counsel, newly discovered evidence, prosecutorial misconduct, or other grounds. Across the United States, there are tens of thousands of pending habeas petitions. Many are filed by pro se petitioners, meaning people representing themselves without a lawyer. These filings are often poorly formatted, legally unsophisticated, and filled with vague references to the trial record. But each one still requires a judge or staff attorney to review the full trial record, evaluate the claims against that record, and determine whether any claim has merit. This creates an enormous backlog measured in years, not months. Meanwhile, real constitutional violations sit unreviewed. Innocent people remain in prison. Lawyers who missed obvious defenses are never identified. The Michigan Supreme Court likely chose Learned Hand in significant part to address exactly this problem. An AI system that can ingest a full trial record, cross-reference it against the petition's claims, flag potentially meritorious issues, and generate a preliminary analysis memo saves hundreds of hours per case. The stakes could not be higher: these cases involve people's freedom. And the architecture's emphasis on never making the decision, only flagging and analyzing, is constitutionally essential in this context. Every petitioner has the right to have their claims actually considered by a human being. The AI accelerates that consideration. It does not replace it.

This brings us to the closed universe versus open universe debate, which is the architectural heart of why Learned Hand exists. A closed universe means the system can only reference documents that exist in its curated corpus. If a case is not in the corpus, it cannot be cited. This eliminates the fabrication vector entirely. An open universe, like general-purpose large language models, generates text by predicting likely token sequences. It has no mechanism to distinguish real citations from fabricated ones.

The Stanford evidence makes this concrete. The Magesh et al. study from Stanford, published in the Journal of Empirical Legal Studies in 2025, is the most rigorous empirical assessment of legal AI hallucination rates to date. They found that raw GPT-4 without retrieval grounding hallucinates 58 to 82 percent of the time on legal queries. Lexis Plus AI, the best-performing commercial tool with retrieval-augmented generation, still hallucinated 17 percent of the time. Westlaw AI-Assisted Research hallucinated 33 percent of the time. Even with retrieval-augmented generation, legal AI tools hallucinate 17 to 33 percent of the time. The study's key finding was blunt: the providers' claims of having eliminated or avoided hallucinations are overstated.

For a court system where a single fabricated citation can trigger sanctions, delay proceedings, and erode public trust, even 1 percent is unacceptable. A closed universe makes that fabrication number zero, at the cost of potential incompleteness. But incompleteness is a manageable failure mode for courts. A law clerk who gets a report saying "I found 15 relevant cases but could not locate authority on this specific issue" knows to do additional research. Confabulation is an unmanageable failure mode. A law clerk who gets a report citing a case that does not exist has been actively misled.

Chapter Eleven: Interview Questions and Answers

Now let's practice answering the questions you are likely to face. I am going to frame each one the way it might come at you, and then walk through what you would say. Practice these out loud. The goal is to sound natural, not rehearsed, so know the substance well enough that you can adapt the delivery.

If they ask "Why five agents and not three or seven?" here is what you would say. Five is the Pareto optimum between specialization and coordination overhead. Each agent maps to a distinct cognitive task that requires a different prompting approach. The parser needs to be comprehensive, meaning find everything. The citation verifier needs to be skeptical, meaning challenge every claim. The fact checker needs to be systematic, meaning cross-reference across documents. The synthesizer needs to be concise, meaning rank and prioritize. And the judicial memo writer needs to be formal, meaning communicate for a legal audience. If you go to three agents by combining verification and fact checking, you lose the parallelism that cuts wall-clock time by 40 percent, and you overload a single agent with two different cognitive tasks. If you go to seven or more by splitting fact checking into per-category agents, you add an LLM call for each one, which means three to ten more seconds per call, more failure points, and more coordination overhead. Five agents hit the sweet spot: enough specialization for distinct tasks, few enough for manageable coordination.

If they ask "Why is the orchestrator not an agent?" here is what you would say. The orchestrator makes zero LLM calls. Its behavior is fully deterministic. Given the same agent outputs, the pipeline always produces the same final report. This matters because it means pipeline control flow can be reasoned about, tested, and debugged independently of LLM behavior. If the orchestrator itself made LLM calls, say to decide which agents to invoke or to route findings, the pipeline's behavior would become harder to predict. A single hallucination in the orchestrator could skip critical verification steps entirely. Debugging would require reasoning about both the agents' non-determinism and the orchestrator's non-determinism simultaneously. By keeping the orchestrator deterministic, you isolate non-determinism to the agents, which makes eval reproducible and debugging tractable.

If they ask "What would you change about the architecture?" here is what you would say. Three things in priority order. First, the citation verification backend. Today it relies on LLM parametric knowledge, which means it asks the model whether it has heard of a case rather than querying a legal database. In production, this needs database-backed verification. I have a five-tier model for this: hardcoded case context at the top, then CourtListener API for broad federal coverage, then Caselaw Access Project for comprehensive historical coverage, then commercial APIs like Westlaw or Lexis for gold-standard completeness, and finally LLM parametric knowledge as the lowest-reliability fallback. Second, PDF processing. The current system only handles text input. Real court filings are PDFs, often scanned. You need digital PDF extraction plus OCR for scanned documents. Third, streaming. The current architecture is request-response. For a document that takes 30 seconds to two minutes to process, you want server-sent events pushing real-time status updates so the law clerk can see which agent is currently running.

Now let's move to eval questions.

If they ask "How do you know it works?" here is what you would say. Thirty-one eval assertions across six categories: recall, precision, hallucination, consistency, uncertainty handling, and output structure. The test case has eight planted errors spanning factual, legal, and procedural categories. The system catches six to seven of those eight consistently. But the eval is not just about recall. There is a precision test using a clean document, Smith versus ABC Corp, that contains no errors. The system should find nothing wrong with it, and that is a separate test. There is a grounding check requiring ten-plus character substring matches against source documents, which prevents the eval from rewarding hallucinated evidence. And there is a dual matching strategy: keyword-based deterministic checks for things you can match exactly, plus an LLM-as-judge semantic evaluation for things that might be phrased differently but mean the same thing. Neither method alone is sufficient. Keywords miss paraphrased findings. The LLM judge misses when the system uses wrong terminology for the right concept. Together they cover more ground.

If they ask "How do you prevent overfitting to your test cases?" here is what you would say. Three mechanisms. First, the clean-document precision test is a negative control. If the system only learns to flag errors in the test document, it will start hallucinating errors in clean documents, and that test catches it. Second, git SHA tracking ties every eval run to a specific code state, so you can see trends over time and detect if prompt changes that improve one metric degrade another. Third, the eval architecture is designed as a registry. Adding new test cases with different error types, different practice areas, and different difficulty levels is incremental, not architectural. The real answer to overfitting is more diverse data, and the infrastructure supports that scaling.

Now let's move to production questions.

If they ask "Is this production ready?" here is what you would say. The pipeline architecture is production-ready. The implementation is not. Those are two orthogonal dimensions. The architecture, meaning five specialized agents with typed Pydantic contracts, parallel execution, graceful degradation, and deterministic orchestration, is sound. Adding legal database integration means changing what the citation verifier agent does internally, not how it connects to the rest of the pipeline. What is not production-ready is the citation verification backend, which is the critical gap. Also document format support, which is currently text-only. I have a five-tier production plan with legal database integration as the top priority, followed by document parsing, dynamic fact categories, model upgrade, and observability.

If they ask "How would you handle a thousand documents per day?" here is what you would say. The current in-process parallelism using asyncio gather runs all citations in a single Python process. For high volume, you move to queue-based processing. The orchestrator pushes citation verification tasks to a task queue like Redis or SQS or Celery. Worker processes pull from the queue and process citations independently. This gives you horizontal scaling by adding workers, backpressure through queue depth limits, built-in retry logic, observability through queue metrics, and failure isolation since a worker crash does not affect other workers. The agents are already stateless, meaning they take input and return output with no shared state. That means they are distributable without architectural changes. You could run citation verification on GPU nodes for speed and fact checking on CPU nodes for cost, with the queue coordinating between them. The only true architectural change needed at scale is the single-prompt fact checker. With fifty-page documents, the six-thousand-character truncation loses critical content, so you would need either chunking within the fact checker or a document summarization agent upstream.

Now let's tackle the gotcha questions, because these are where honesty is your strongest weapon.

If they say "Your citation verifier cannot actually verify citations, can it?" here is what you would say. You are right that it relies on parametric knowledge today. It asks the LLM whether it has heard of a case, which is fundamentally different from querying a legal database. For obviously fabricated cases it works, but for real but obscure citations it cannot distinguish between "this case does not exist" and "I have not seen this case in my training data." That said, the architecture is designed for database integration. The verification logic, the status categories of supported, not supported, could not verify, and misleading, the three layers of error handling, the parallel per-citation execution, the Pydantic contracts, all of that is production-ready. The knowledge source needs to be swapped. The case context injection pattern, where domain knowledge is passed into the verification prompt, already works for any case. Today it is nineteen lines of hardcoded context for the Rivera case. In production, that injection point would pull from a curated legal corpus or database API. The verification logic is identical regardless of the source.

If they ask "What if a judge relies on your tool and it's wrong?" here is what you would say. This is why precision matters more than recall in a court-facing system. A false positive, meaning the system says something is wrong when it is not, erodes judicial trust and wastes limited clerk time. A false negative, meaning the system misses a real error, is also bad, but it is the status quo since law clerks already miss things. The system is designed to express uncertainty through the could-not-verify status. Without that status, the model would be forced to choose between supported, which is potentially wrong, and not supported, which is potentially unfair to the attorney. Could-not-verify is the honest middle ground. The prompt has explicit precision rules: when in doubt, prefer consistent or could-not-verify over contradictory. This encodes the principle that it is better to say "I am not sure" than to give a wrong answer. In production with a legal database, items that return could-not-verify would genuinely indicate a problem, since the case might not exist in any database. From an LLM alone, could-not-verify just means "I do not have enough information," which is honest.

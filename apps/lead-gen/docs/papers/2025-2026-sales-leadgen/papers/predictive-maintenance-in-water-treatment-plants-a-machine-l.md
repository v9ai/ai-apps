# Predictive Maintenance in Water Treatment Plants: A Machine Learning-Based Fault Diagnosis and Optimization Approach

**Authors.** Paniteja Madala

**Venue / year.** International Conference Electronic Systems, Signal Processing and Computing Technologies [ICESC-] · 2025

**Links.** [source](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/712d8c9b447bfa5ee84102e4241d5c7bce496fb0)

**Abstract.**

Water treatment plants are critical pieces of infrastructure that create an increased reliance on the functionality of mechanical and chemical components such as pumps, valves, and sensors. Inexplicable failures in any of the numerous parts or subsystems can lead to costly downtime, reduced operational efficiency, and compromised water quality. In this paper, a machine learning-based predictive maintenance architecture to detect faults and improve system reliability in water treatment plants is proposed. For simulation and training, a synthetic dataset was generated to mimic real sensor readings including pressure, flow rate, vibration, temperature, pH, and RPM. To mitigate the class imbalance commonly found in fault detection datasets, the Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) was applied to the training data. The classification was done with the XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) algorithm, known for its high accuracy, resilience, and interpretability. The model was trained on SMOTE-oversampled data and assessed using standard performance metrics such as Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-Score, and the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) Curve. An F1 score of 0.71, accuracy of $\mathbf{7 2 \%}$, and ROC-AUC of $\mathbf{0. 8 1}$ were achieved through the experiments, which suggest a high fault detection ability. Vibration and pressure turned out to be the two most significant factors for equipment deterioration, according to the feature importance analysis. The resulting framework establishes normal and faulty operational conditions for efficient maintenance scheduling and system optimization. In doing so, this work creates a methodological roadmap for the incorporation of smart monitoring functionality into water treatment systems. A fault diagnostic system (FDM) that is scalable, dependable, and explainable for real-time analytics in Smart Water Infrastructure. Further work entails deployment of the framework on live SCADA data, examination of deep-learning time-series models, and embedding with predictive systems in Digital Twin (DT) ecosystems.

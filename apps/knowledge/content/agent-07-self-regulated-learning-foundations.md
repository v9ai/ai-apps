# Foundational Self-Regulated Learning (SRL) with AI: Research Landscape (2018-2026)

## Executive Summary

This research landscape analysis examines foundational self-regulated learning (SRL) theories, models, and their integration with artificial intelligence in educational contexts from 2018-2026. The analysis covers SRL theoretical frameworks, metacognition, motivation theories, goal setting, time management, and measurement approaches, with particular focus on AI-enhanced education applications.

## 1. Foundational SRL Models and Theoretical Frameworks

### 1.1 Zimmerman's Cyclical Model
Barry Zimmerman's cyclical model of SRL remains foundational, conceptualizing learning as a three-phase cyclical process:
- **Forethought Phase**: Task analysis, goal setting, strategic planning
- **Performance Phase**: Self-control, self-observation, strategy implementation
- **Self-Reflection Phase**: Self-judgment, self-evaluation, causal attribution

### 1.2 Winne's COPES Model
Philip Winne's COPES model (Conditions, Operations, Products, Evaluations, Standards) emphasizes:
- **Conditions**: Task conditions and cognitive conditions
- **Operations**: Cognitive operations during learning
- **Products**: Cognitive products generated
- **Evaluations**: Monitoring against standards
- **Standards**: Internal and external criteria for evaluation

### 1.3 Pintrich's Framework
Paul Pintrich's framework organizes SRL across four areas:
- **Cognition**: Rehearsal, elaboration, organization strategies
- **Motivation**: Self-efficacy, task value, goal orientation
- **Behavior**: Time management, effort regulation
- **Context**: Environmental structuring, help-seeking

**Key Reference**: **Panadero (2017)** [A Review of Self-regulated Learning: Six Models and Four Directions for Research](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2017.00422) - Comprehensive review of six major SRL models with 2,276+ citations.

## 3. Motivation Theories in Digital Learning

### 3.1 Self-Determination Theory (SDT)
- **Autonomy**: Learner control and choice in learning activities
- **Competence**: Mastery experiences and skill development
- **Relatedness**: Social connections and belongingness

### 3.2 Expectancy-Value Theory
- **Expectancy**: Beliefs about ability to succeed
- **Value**: Perceived importance and utility of learning
- **Cost**: Perceived barriers and sacrifices

### 3.3 Achievement Goal Theory
- **Mastery Goals**: Focus on learning and improvement
- **Performance Goals**: Focus on demonstrating ability
- **Approach vs. Avoidance**: Orientation toward success vs. avoiding failure

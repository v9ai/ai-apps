## Search Intent
Informational with high curiosity — the idea of an AI editing its own prompts is inherently fascinating. Searchers want to understand both the mechanism and the safety constraints. The "How We Built" format promises a practical walkthrough.

## Competitive Landscape
| Rank | Title | Domain | Format | Word Count | Gap |

## Recommended Structure
- **Format**: How-to / build log with architecture decisions
- **Word count**: 1500-2000
- **Title tag**: "How We Built an Agent That Edits Its Own Instructions"
- **Meta description**: "An AI agent that improves its own prompts based on measured evidence — not vibes. Five research papers, five anti-patterns, and one strict scope boundary that makes it safe."
- **H1**: How We Built an Agent That Edits Its Own Instructions
- **H2s**:
  1. The Self-Modification Problem — why agents should (and shouldn't) edit their own prompts
  2. Five Papers That Shaped the Design — Meta Context Engineering, CASTER, REprompt, EvoConfig, Autonomous Question Formation
  3. Evidence-Based Evolution — every change links to data
  4. The Five Anti-Patterns — instruction bloat, contradictions, over-specificity, prompt theater, cargo cult
  5. The Safety Boundary — what it can and cannot edit

## Internal Linking Opportunities
- Trajectory Miner article → provides the evidence the Skill Evolver acts on
- Verification Gate article → validates the Skill Evolver's changes

## Differentiation Strategy
"Agent that edits its own prompts" is a hook nobody else is writing about with a working implementation. The anti-patterns section is especially valuable — most content about self-improving AI is aspirational; ours shows what goes wrong.

## Distribution Notes
- HackerNews: "self-modifying AI prompts" triggers strong opinions — high engagement
- Reddit r/MachineLearning: the anti-patterns are shareable
- Twitter/X: thread the 5 anti-patterns as standalone insights
